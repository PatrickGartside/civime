Hawaii law gives you specific rights to see government records and attend public meetings. Two laws form the foundation of government transparency in the state — and both are administered by the same office. This page explains what they are, how to use them, and what to do when things go wrong.

This is a plain-language overview. For the full legal text, see the linked statutes below. For help with a specific situation, contact the Office of Information Practices.

The Sunshine Law — your right to attend public meetings

The Sunshine Law (Hawaii Revised Statutes Chapter 92) is Hawaii's open meetings law. It says that when a government board, commission, or council meets to discuss public business, you have the right to be there.

What the Sunshine Law requires

  • Advance notice. Meeting agendas must be posted at least six calendar days before the meeting.
  • Published agendas. The notice must list every item to be discussed. A board can't discuss something that wasn't on the agenda.
  • Public testimony. You have the right to testify on agenda items at public meetings.
  • Public votes. Votes must happen in the open. No secret ballots.
  • Published minutes. The board must keep a written record of what happened, and that record is public.

What the Sunshine Law does not cover

  • Informal gatherings where no decisions are made and less than a quorum (majority of members) is present
  • Internal staff meetings — the law applies to boards and commissions, not day-to-day agency operations
  • The Legislature — the Hawaii State Legislature operates under its own rules, not the Sunshine Law (though legislative hearings are still open to the public)

Who enforces it

The Office of Information Practices (OIP) administers the Sunshine Law. If you believe a meeting was improperly closed to the public, or an agenda item was discussed without proper notice, you can contact OIP (see below).

UIPA — your right to see government records

The Uniform Information Practices Act (HRS Chapter 92F) — called UIPA — is Hawaii's public records law. Think of it as Hawaii's version of the federal Freedom of Information Act (FOIA), but for state and county government.

In plain terms: you can ask any state or county agency for their records, and they have to give them to you — unless a specific legal exemption applies.

What you can request

Almost any government record. This includes documents, emails, reports, data, meeting minutes, contracts, correspondence, and more. If a government agency created it or received it as part of their work, it's likely a public record.

How to make a request

Put it in writing. Email works fine — no special form required. Be as specific as you can about what you're looking for.

Include:

  • Your name and email address
  • A description of the records you want
  • A date range, if relevant
  • How you'd like to receive them (email is fastest)

Sample records request

You can copy and adapt this example:

Subject: UIPA Records Request

Dear [Agency Name],

Under the Uniform Information Practices Act (HRS Chapter 92F),
I am requesting the following records:

[Describe what you want — for example: "Meeting minutes from
the Board of Water Supply for January through March 2026"]

Please provide these records by email if possible.

Thank you,
[Your Name]
[Your Email]

You don't need to explain why you want a record. You don't need a lawyer. You don't need to cite a specific law. A short email is all it takes.

Fees

Agencies may charge reasonable copying fees for paper copies, but they cannot charge you for records delivered by email. If you request email delivery, there should be no cost.

What if your request is denied?

If an agency refuses your request — or doesn't respond within a reasonable time — you can appeal to the Office of Information Practices. OIP will review the denial and issue a formal opinion. This process is free.

The Office of Information Practices (OIP)

OIP is the state office that administers both the Sunshine Law and UIPA. They're the people to call when something isn't working the way it should.

What OIP does

  • Provides guidance to agencies and the public on open meetings and records access
  • Issues formal opinions when disputes arise
  • Conducts training for government employees
  • Publishes annual reports on government transparency
  • Maintains the Records Report System — a searchable database of over 26,000 types of government records

When to contact OIP

  • Your records request was denied or ignored
  • A meeting was closed to the public without legal justification
  • An agenda item was discussed without proper notice
  • You have a question about your rights under either law

Contact OIP

  • Phone: (808) 586-1400
  • Email: oip@hawaii.gov
  • Website: oip.hawaii.gov

Other government transparency resources

Beyond open meetings and public records, several other offices help you follow the money, track elections, and access government information.

  • Hawaii State Ethics Commission — Financial disclosures, lobbyist registrations, and conflict of interest guidance. Phone: (808) 587-0460
  • Campaign Spending Commission — Campaign contributions and expenditures for state and county candidates
  • Hawaii State Legislature — Bills, hearing schedules, and testimony submission
  • eHawaii Public Meeting Calendar — Official state meeting calendar
  • Office of Elections — Voter registration, election dates, and results
